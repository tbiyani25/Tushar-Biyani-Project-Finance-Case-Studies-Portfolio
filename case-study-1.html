Section 01

Executive Summary

Investment Thesis: The project delivers an attractive, resilient equity return underpinned by a 20-year CPI-indexed CfD at £94.1/MWh (2024 prices). The P50 base case delivers a 10.4% equity IRR and 1.57× cash-on-cash multiple on the 50% co-investment, supported by a clear PE value-creation path through a £739m refinancing dividend at Commercial Operational Date plus 1 year (Q1 2030) and £2,781m exit at 12.0× EBITDA valuation into the institutional buyer pool in Q1 2034 with 15 years of CfD remaining. The downside protection is strong, with P75 IRR holding at 6.4% while returns only turn negative under a 1-in-100 year P99 outcome, rather than a realistic operating case.

Return Drivers & Sensitivities: Construction Capex is the dominant value driver generating a 13.4% IRR swing across the tested range (£2,148–£3,063/kW), materially exceeding all other sensitivities. This highlights the importance of mitigating Capex escalation through mechanisms such as fixed-price EPC contracts, robust liquidated damages provisions or tighter contractor risk allocation. Contingency sizing is equally critical, with an increase from 2.5% to 10% reducing P50 IRR to 7.1% and pushing P90 returns negative, reinforcing that contingency should be treated as a strategic investment decision rather than a mechanical buffer. Opex represents the second-largest downside risk, with higher-than-expected O&M and Connection & UoS charges reducing returns by ~6%, although a significant portion can be mitigated through contractual lock-ins. By contrast, interest rate sensitivity is minimal (0.3% swing in IRR), confirming that execution discipline, procurement strategy, and Opex certainty are materially more important than marginal financing optimisation.

Financing Robustness & Lender Perspective: Debt is appropriately structured and well-sculpted to the project's contracted cash flows, with operational gearing of 62–69% across the P-curve, median DSCR stable at ~1.30×, and minimum DSCR greater than 1.10× in all scenarios bar one extreme downside case (1.07× in P99 with 2.5% electricity generation escalation). Importantly, that breach is structural and timing-driven rather than fundamental. Debt sculpting amortises substantially over the 20-year CfD term, leaving residual balances below £100m at CfD expiry date under all scenarios. Overall, the financing structure demonstrates strong resilience, supported by a 6-month forward DSRA, MMRA from Year 5, and ring-fenced decommissioning security, resulting in robust lender protections and limited downside credit risk.

Key Investment & Financing Takeaways
  • Construction Capex is the dominant value driver: 13.4% IRR swing across the Capex range, mitigated through fixed-price EPC contracts, robust liquidated damages and tight risk allocation. Capex risk is borne by equity, not debt.
  • Contingency sizing is itself a core investment decision: Increase from 2.5% to 10% contingency cuts P50 IRR by 3.3% to 7.1% and pushes P90 IRR negative. Contingency should be carefully sized to the actual risk envelope rather than accepted as a mechanical buffer.
  • Opex requires contractual lock-in: Adverse O&M plus Connection & UoS charges reduce IRR to ~4%, with costs compounding via CPI/RPI indexation over the asset life. The highest uncertainty remains for Connection & UoS charges.
  • Refinancing proceeds are the largest single contributor to early IRR: Proceeds of £739m (base) vary by +30%/–40% across scenarios, driven primarily by Capex and price escalation. Securing lender appetite to re-lever to ~69% at refinancing is therefore a strategic priority.
  • Terminal value is stable: It ranges from £2,594m–£2,976m (~7% spread around the £2,781m base), supported by a high valuation multiple due to an exit with 15 years of CfD remaining.
  • DSCR is robust across the realistic P-curve: Minimum DSCR sits at >1.10× in every scenario except the P99 + 2.5% escalation case (1.07×), which is timing-driven rather than fundamental. A cash sweep or cash trap of £48–78m in the final 2–3 years of debt is required to guarantee full repayment ahead of merchant tail transition.
  • Quarterly DSCR weakness is seasonal and self-correcting: Lower DSCR pressure is concentrated in Q2 (low-wind season) during the first six years and recovers to ~1.30× thereafter as debt amortises through high-wind Q1 and Q4 periods.

Norfolk Vanguard West is a 1.5 GW offshore wind project being jointly developed by RWE and KKR as part of the wider Norfolk Offshore Wind Zone in the UK North Sea. The project secured a 20-year CfD in Allocation Round 7 and is expected to reach commercial operations by approximately Q1 2029.

The financial model assesses the project bankability and evaluates return from equity investor perspective. Developed in Microsoft Excel, the model comprises two core modules: (i) a construction module covering Capex deployment, construction debt drawdown, interest during construction, and debt repayment schedule, and (ii) an operations module covering energy yield, CfD revenues, Opex, debt sizing and sculpting, DSCR analysis, reserve accounts and cash flow waterfall mechanics. Sensitivity analysis was performed across key Capex and Opex assumptions to assess the impact on equity IRR, minimum DSCR, and cash on cash multiple.

Section 03

Key Takeaways from the Model

Private Equity Perspective

Equity returns, value drivers & risk factors

Model base case results for all scenarios (P50 to P99)

Base case delivers attractive, resilient infrastructure returns:

The P50 base case generates a 10.4% unlevered IRR and a 1.57× cash-on-cash multiple, positioning the project firmly within the target return range for private equity (infrastructure) perspective. Returns remain robust under downside scenarios as at P75, the IRR is 6.4%, still above typical institutional hurdle rates for contracted assets. Material downside is limited to extreme wind scenarios where the return only turn negative at P99, representing a 1-in-100-year outcome rather than a realistic operational case.
The project offers strong base-case returns with defensible downside protection, supported by contracted revenues and limited exposure to all but extreme resource risk.
Change in equity IRR for P50 scenario (percentage points vs 10.4% base case)

Construction Capex is the dominant value driver:

Capex drives the widest IRR range, from 16.4% down to 3.0%, representing a 13.4% swing across the Capex range (£2,148–£3,063/kW). No other variable has comparable impact.
Controlling construction cost risk is critical to protecting infrastructure return. This should be actively mitigated through either or a combination of fixed-price EPC contracts for major packages (e.g. turbines, foundations), robust liquidated damages provisions and tight contractor risk allocation. These measures are fundamental to protecting the downside on equity returns.

Opex is a secondary but still material downside risk (~6% IRR impact):

In a high-cost scenario, combined O&M plus Connection & UoS costs reduce IRR to ~4%, materially eroding value. O&M costs also compound over the operational lifecycle of the wind farm (from increases from CPI/RPI) and small annual cost increases can become material.
Costs should be carefully managed, particularly O&M costs should be contractually locked and indexed appropriately to limit exposure where as Connection & UoS costs will initially remain uncertain (until OFTO is confirmed), representing a residual risk that needs to be factored into model.

Electricity rate escalation is another important upside/downside lever:

A 0.5% increase in escalation (2.0% to 2.5%) drives a 3.4% IRR uplift (to ~13.8% P50), while a –0.5% decrease reduces IRR by ~3.1% (to ~7.3%). This means revenue assumptions are highly sensitive to long-term price growth.
There are some protections that can be put in place to limit the impact. For example the CfD structure provides partial downside protection as the awarded strike price is CPI-indexed over the 20-year term, providing a built-in inflation hedge (historically ~2% p.a. compounding) and additional downside mitigation could be achieved via PPAs that incorporate fixed annual price escalation (e.g. ~2%) and/or explicit inflation indexation. Therefore structuring the offtaker strategy is a key lever to stabilise long-term returns.

Interest rate sensitivity is low within the tested range:

A +/– 25 to 35bps movement in construction debt, first lien, or second lien pricing results in only a +/– 0.2% impact on IRR. This means returns are relatively insensitive to marginal changes in financing costs and immaterial to equity returns.
Therefore optimisation effort is better focused on material cost drivers that have meaningful IRR impact such as securing competitive EPC pricing or locking in Opex certainty and guarantees.
Contingency impact on the IRR across all scenarios (P50 to P99)

Contingency sizing is a core investment lever:

Increasing contingency from 2.5% to 10% (a reasonable Pre-FID range considered through an additional test) reduces P50 IRR by 3.3% to 7.1% and drives P90 IRR into negative territory. This highlights that contingency has a direct and material impact on equity return.
Contingency sizing must be treated as a core investment decision, not a mechanical buffer because over-provisioning materially erodes returns and under-provisioning increases execution and cost overrun risk. Furthermore active management of construction Capex is critical, and could include tight project cost control (including change flags) and procurement discipline, mitigating price escalation risk through contracting strategy (e.g. fixed pricing wherever possible, pass through of costs to suppliers, liquidated damages provisions) and ensuring contingency reflects a risk-adjusted, evidence-based view rather than generic benchmarks.
Changes in terminal value for P50 scenarios

Terminal value is relatively stable across scenarios:

Terminal value ranges from £2,594m (low electricity escalation) to £2,976m (high electricity escalation), representing a spread of ~7% around the £2,781m base case.
Terminal value is not a primary source of volatility. The limited spread indicates that underlying EBITDA remains relatively stable across scenarios when valued on a consistent exit multiple basis. This stability reflects strong, contracted early-year cash flows, with exit occurring within the first five years of operations. Additionally, both revenues and operational costs are linked to CPI/RPI, supporting cash flow viability beyond the initial exit horizon for the next equity investor.
Changes in net refinancing proceeds for P50 scenario

Refinancing proceeds are a key source of value but highly sensitive:

Net refinancing proceeds vary significantly, ranging from +30% to –40% relative to the £739m base case, driven primarily by construction Capex and power price escalation assumptions.
Refinancing is a major driver of equity value, given its earlier timing and therefore higher contribution to IRR versus terminal value or operating cash flows. It also represents a substantial portion of cash returned to investors, making it a critical component of the investment case. The high sensitivity means that value can erode quickly under adverse scenarios and therefore Capex discipline is critical and overruns materially impact overall returns.
Lenders Perspective

Debt service coverage, downside resilience & covenant headroom

Operations period debt repayment profile for base case

Debt amortises steadily across all scenarios with no cliff-edge refinancing risk:

The debt sculpting methodology is based on the present value of forecasted CFADS using the weighted average interest rate of the first and second lien debt, with total debt allocated across the tranche structure accordingly.
Across all scenarios (P50 to P99), the residual debt balance at CfD expiry falls below £100m, indicating that the financing structure is capable of materially amortising debt over the contracted revenue period despite quarterly variability in wind generation.
The combination of minimum generation strike price and inflation-linked revenues, inflation linked Opex, disciplined debt sculpting, and the debt service reserve account (forward looking of 6 months) provides a highly predictable debt repayment profile across the wind farm's operations period. While offshore wind revenues are inherently seasonal, the structure effectively smooths cash flow volatility and supports stable debt servicing capacity.
Given a modest residual balance remains at the end of the CfD term, lenders should consider either a cash sweep and/or a cash trap mechanism in the final 2–3 years of the debt tenor to ensure full debt payment by the CfD expiry date.

DSCR remains robust across scenarios (P50 to P90):

DSCR is above the 1.10× minimum in all scenarios except one extreme case (P99). This demonstrates strong lender protection across the downside range. Capex does not impact DSCR, as it affects equity returns whereas the key drivers of DSCR are Opex, interest rates and generation profile (degradation, and price escalation).
Lenders should seek to maximise certainty over controllable variables, including fixing or tightly managing Opex through contractual arrangements and locking in financing terms (rather than linking this with the benchmark market interest rates which can move up and down during the CfD period of the offshore wind farm).

Isolated DSCR covenant breach driven by structuring dynamics (not fundamental weakness):

The only DSCR breach (~1.07×) occurs in a P99 scenario with higher electricity escalation (2.5%), and is temporary and structural in nature.
This is driven because debt is sized on lifetime cash flows (LLCR), which are higher under stronger price escalation assumptions. This allows for a larger overall debt quantum, increasing interest payments (particularly during the early years where little to no debt is amortised). However, cash flow (CFADS) uplift from escalation is back-ended, while interest costs are front-loaded.
A similar dynamic is observed with lower annual degradation assumption, where stronger lifetime generation supports higher debt sizing, again increasing early-period interest burden.
The DSCR profile is structurally sound, with only a temporary and explainable breach in an extreme downside case. The issue is driven by debt sizing methodology and timing effects, not underlying asset performance. Overall, the project provides robust credit metrics with limited lender downside risk.
Gearing ratio from the most conservative scenarios

Debt Is Appropriately Structured for the Risk Profile:

Operational gearing ranges from ~62% to 69% across P50 and P90 scenarios, with construction gearing fixed at 65%. This reflects a balanced and conservative capital structure for a contracted offshore wind asset. Median DSCR remains stable at ~1.3×, with variability driven primarily by the minimum DSCR in specific periods, rather than a deterioration in overall credit quality.
This shows debt service pressure is not material to the project, but timing-driven as debt stress is concentrated in early operational periods, when wind resource is seasonally lower (e.g. summer quarters), debt balances and therefore interest costs are at their peak and amortisation has not yet materially reduced leverage. Outside of these periods (first 6 years of operation of the offshore wind farm), cash flow coverage is strong and stable, indicating that the financing structure is well aligned with the asset's cash flow profile.
The financing is robust and appropriately calibrated, with temporary, timing-related pressure points rather than structural weaknesses in the debt package.
Minimum DSCR is met for all 4 quarters after 6 years of operation

DSCR covenant is met in all quarters from year 6 of operations onwards:

Quarterly DSCR pressure is concentrated in Q2, driven by seasonally lower wind generation in line with the project's production seasonality profile, where Q2 represents the weakest generation period of the year. This results in the lowest CFADS and in early years, interest payments exceed the ideal sculpted profile in this period, temporarily compressing DSCR.
However, as debt amortises during high wind generation periods (Q4 and Q1) and revenues increase due to CPI indexation, the outstanding debt balance and associated interest burden reduce over time resulting in the DSCR recovering quickly to ~1.3× within the first six years post the refinancing period.
The temporary DSCR weakness is driven by seasonality and front-loaded debt balances, rather than any structural weakness in the project's cash flow profile or financing structure.
